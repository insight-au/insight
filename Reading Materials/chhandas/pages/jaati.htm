Jaati is that class of metres, where the classification is done on the basis of the time taken to recite a quarter. Such metres are also called Maatraachandas. Here, the syllables are are still divided into short and long, but the classification is based on the m:a*:a (maatraa -syllabic instants) they have.

        A maatraa is defined as the time taken to recite a short syllable. Technically, it is the time taken to snap the fingers or to blink the eyes once. A short syllable is said to have one maatraa, and the long syllable, two. In case of a maatraavrttam, to find out the name of the metre, we find the short and long syllables first, and then calculate the number of maatraas.

        There are many varieties in 'jaati', but we shall see only the very popular ones used widely in the various Sanskrit works. The most widely used one is A:y:aü (aaryaa).The verse below describes the maatraa scheme for aaryaa :

Another widely used kind is g:iet: (geeti). This is similar to aaryaa. If the rules applied to the first two quarters of aaryaa is applied again to the next two quarters, we get geeti. That is, it has 12 maatraas in the I and III quarters, and 18 in the II and IV.

One more metre of this kind is the m::a*:as:m:km:Î (maatraasamakam). The general definition for this is that the metre must contain 16 maatraas in each quarter, irrespective of the pattern of syllables. Though there are many varieties of this metre, each adding its own refinenment to this rule, we shall not discuss them.
